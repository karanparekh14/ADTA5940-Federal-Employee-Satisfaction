## Introduction

The rapid expansion of remote work across the federal workforce, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, has fundamentally reshaped how government agencies attract, retain, and engage employees. As agencies continue to refine their telework policies amid political and operational pressures, understanding the relationship between remote work arrangements, work-life balance, and employee satisfaction has become a critical priority for public-sector human resource management. This literature review synthesizes scholarly research and industry reports published primarily between 2020 and 2025 to establish the theoretical and empirical foundation for analyzing federal employee job satisfaction using the 2024 Federal Employee Viewpoint Survey (FEVS). The review is organized into four thematic areas: (1) the evolution of telework in the federal government, (2) theoretical frameworks linking remote work to employee outcomes, (3) empirical findings on work-life balance and job satisfaction, and (4) the role of employee engagement as a mediating factor.

## The Evolution of Telework in the Federal Government

Telework in the U.S. federal government has a legislative history that predates the pandemic. The Telework Enhancement Act of 2010 required each executive agency to establish telework policies and designate a Telework Managing Officer (U.S. Office of Personnel Management [OPM], 2023). However, adoption remained uneven across agencies, with many supervisors reluctant to approve remote arrangements. The COVID-19 pandemic served as an inflection point, forcing rapid adoption of maximum telework across nearly all federal agencies beginning in March 2020 (Makarius et al., 2021). According to OPM's annual telework reports, the percentage of federal employees engaged in some form of telework increased from approximately 40% pre-pandemic to over 60% during the height of the pandemic (OPM, 2023).

As agencies transitioned from emergency operations back to routine postures, debate intensified around how much telework should persist. Congressional hearings and executive orders in 2023 and 2024 emphasized returning federal workers to offices, while employee advocacy groups argued that telework improved both morale and productivity (Government Accountability Office [GAO], 2024). The 2024 FEVS results provide a timely dataset for examining how these evolving policies relate to employee satisfaction and engagement on a national scale.

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Job Demands-Resources Theory

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model (Bakker & Demerouti, 2017) provides a widely used theoretical lens for understanding how workplace conditions influence employee well-being and satisfaction. The JD-R model posits that every job has demands (aspects that require sustained effort and are associated with physiological or psychological costs) and resources (aspects that help achieve work goals, reduce demands, or stimulate personal growth). Telework can function as a job resource by offering employees greater autonomy and schedule flexibility, thereby reducing commuting demands and enabling better management of personal responsibilities (Gajendran & Harrison, 2007). When job resources exceed demands, employees experience higher engagement and satisfaction; when demands dominate, burnout and dissatisfaction follow.

### Self-Determination Theory

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) (Deci & Ryan, 2000) complements the JD-R framework by explaining the psychological mechanisms through which autonomy influences motivation and satisfaction. SDT holds that three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — must be satisfied for individuals to experience intrinsic motivation and well-being. Remote work directly enhances perceived autonomy by giving employees control over their work environment and schedule. However, it may simultaneously threaten relatedness if employees feel isolated from colleagues and supervisors. The balance of these effects likely determines whether telework ultimately improves or diminishes satisfaction (Wang et al., 2021).

### Spillover Theory

Spillover theory (Edwards & Rothbard, 2000) explains the bidirectional relationship between work and personal life domains. Positive spillover occurs when satisfaction or skills in one domain transfer beneficially to the other, while negative spillover occurs when stress or conflict in one domain impairs functioning in the other. Telework may promote positive spillover by enabling employees to attend to family responsibilities without sacrificing work productivity. Conversely, blurred boundaries between work and home can generate negative spillover if employees struggle to disconnect (Allen et al., 2015). Work-life balance perceptions, therefore, serve as a critical mediating mechanism between telework arrangements and job satisfaction.

## Remote Work and Job Satisfaction: Empirical Evidence

A growing body of empirical research has examined the telework-satisfaction link across organizational settings. Bloom et al. (2015), in a randomized controlled trial at a Chinese travel agency, found that employees assigned to work from home experienced a 13% performance increase and reported substantially higher job satisfaction and lower turnover intentions compared to office-based counterparts. While this study predates the pandemic and is set in the private sector, it remains one of the most rigorous causal evaluations of remote work effects.

More recently, Kaduk et al. (2019) analyzed data from a large U.S. employer and found that telework frequency was positively associated with job satisfaction but that this relationship was moderated by supervisor support — employees whose managers actively supported remote work reported the highest satisfaction. This finding underscores the importance of organizational culture beyond mere policy availability.

In the federal context, Caillier (2012) used earlier waves of the FEVS to examine telework and satisfaction among federal employees, finding a positive association between telework participation and global job satisfaction. Extending this work, Caillier (2016) demonstrated that the telework-satisfaction relationship was partially mediated by role clarity, suggesting that telework benefits satisfaction most when employees have well-defined responsibilities.

The post-pandemic landscape has introduced new dynamics. Barrero et al. (2023) analyzed nationally representative data and found that employees who worked from home valued the arrangement at approximately 8% of salary equivalent, with the primary benefit being reduced commuting time and improved work-life flexibility. Notably, hybrid arrangements (2-3 days per week) appeared to optimize satisfaction, while full-time remote work showed diminishing returns for some employees due to social isolation. Similarly, Bloom (2024) reported findings from a large-scale hybrid work experiment showing that hybrid schedules reduced attrition by 33% and had no negative impact on performance or promotion rates, further supporting the satisfaction benefits of flexible arrangements.

Within the federal government specifically, Lee and Kim (2023) examined the 2022 FEVS and found that routine teleworkers reported significantly higher satisfaction scores than onsite-required employees, even after controlling for agency, occupation, and demographics. However, the authors noted a potential endogeneity concern: employees who are permitted to telework may disproportionately belong to higher-grade, higher-autonomy positions that independently predict satisfaction.

## Work-Life Balance as a Mediating Mechanism

The literature consistently identifies work-life balance (WLB) as a key mechanism through which telework influences satisfaction. Greenhaus and Allen (2011) defined work-life balance as the degree to which an individual's effectiveness and satisfaction in work and family roles are compatible with the individual's life priorities. Employees who achieve balance report lower stress, fewer interpersonal conflicts, and higher overall life satisfaction, which in turn spills over into job satisfaction.

Shockley and Allen (2007) provided meta-analytic evidence that schedule flexibility — a direct consequence of telework access — was one of the strongest predictors of work-family balance. More recently, Vaziri et al. (2020) studied the transition to remote work during the pandemic and found that employees who successfully established boundaries between work and non-work activities experienced significant WLB improvements and corresponding satisfaction gains. However, those who experienced work-home interference — checking emails during family time or handling childcare during meetings — reported decreased balance and lower satisfaction, illustrating that telework is not inherently beneficial without effective boundary management.

In the federal workforce, questions on the FEVS directly capture WLB perceptions. Items such as "My supervisor supports my need to balance work and other life issues" (Q49) and "Senior leaders demonstrate support for Work-Life programs" (Q63) reflect the organizational dimension of WLB that extends beyond individual telework arrangements. Fernandez et al. (2021) analyzed FEVS data and found that supervisor and organizational support for WLB were among the strongest predictors of job satisfaction, even stronger than telework participation itself. This finding suggests that the organizational culture around flexibility may matter more than the flexible arrangement per se.

## Employee Engagement: The Dominant Predictor

Employee engagement has emerged as the most robust predictor of job satisfaction in both the private and public sectors. The OPM defines employee engagement through its Employee Engagement Index (EEI), which encompasses three subindices: Leaders Lead (perceptions of senior leadership integrity and communication), Supervisors (quality of supervisory relationships), and Intrinsic Work Experience (feelings of accomplishment, empowerment, and skill utilization) (OPM, 2024).

Harter et al. (2002) conducted a foundational meta-analysis across 7,939 business units and found that employee engagement was significantly related to customer satisfaction, productivity, profit, employee turnover, and safety outcomes. Within the public sector, Lavigna (2013) demonstrated that engaged federal employees reported substantially higher satisfaction and commitment, and that agencies with higher engagement scores had lower attrition rates and higher performance ratings.

The relationship between engagement and satisfaction is theoretically bidirectional but practically operates as a reinforcing cycle: engaged employees invest discretionary effort in their work, experience mastery and meaning, and consequently report higher satisfaction, which in turn sustains engagement (Saks, 2006). For the current study, the EEI serves as a critical control variable because it captures the quality of the leadership environment and work experience that may confound the telework-satisfaction relationship.

Recent federal workforce analyses have highlighted a potential Simpson's Paradox in the telework-satisfaction data: raw comparisons may show that in-office employees have lower satisfaction, but this difference could be driven by underlying differences in engagement and organizational culture rather than by telework itself (Techera et al., 2016). Controlling for engagement allows isolation of the independent telework effect, which prior research suggests is modest once engagement is accounted for.

## Demographic Considerations

Demographic characteristics such as age, gender, supervisory status, and organizational tenure may moderate the relationship between telework and satisfaction. Older workers and those with longer tenure may have well-established work routines and professional networks that make them less dependent on telework for satisfaction (Morganson et al., 2010). Supervisors may experience telework differently than non-supervisors because managing remote teams introduces unique challenges around coordination and trust (Golden & Fromen, 2011). Gender differences in telework satisfaction have been documented, with women often reporting greater WLB benefits from telework due to disproportionate caregiving responsibilities, though these effects may be context-dependent (Lott & Chung, 2016).

The FEVS provides demographic variables including age group, gender, supervisory status, and federal tenure that allow examination of these moderating effects. Including demographics as a final block in a hierarchical regression model allows assessment of whether the telework, WLB, and engagement effects are robust across demographic subgroups or are artifacts of compositional differences in who teleworks.

## Summary and Research Gap

The existing literature establishes several key findings: (1) telework is generally associated with higher job satisfaction, but the magnitude of this effect varies by context, supervisor support, and boundary management effectiveness; (2) work-life balance is a primary mechanism linking telework to satisfaction; (3) employee engagement is the dominant predictor of satisfaction in workplace studies and may confound simple telework-satisfaction comparisons; and (4) demographic factors can moderate these relationships.

However, several gaps remain. Most post-pandemic studies of federal telework rely on the 2020-2022 FEVS waves, collected during or immediately after pandemic emergency operations. The 2024 FEVS captures a more stable policy environment following the full return-to-office debates of 2023-2024, providing a timely update. Additionally, few studies have used hierarchical regression to simultaneously decompose the telework effect after controlling for WLB, engagement, and demographics in a single integrated model. The present study addresses these gaps by applying a four-block hierarchical OLS regression to the complete 2024 FEVS public-release dataset (N > 500,000), allowing precise estimation of each predictor block's incremental contribution to explaining federal employee job satisfaction.
